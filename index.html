The Stages of Life

The predictable patterns of growth from conception to late adulthood.

Infancy

The Foundation (0-2 Years)

Rapid physical growth, foundational sensory learning, and the critical formation of attachment to caregivers.

  • Physical: Dramatic growth in height and weight; development of basic motor skills like crawling and walking.
  • Mental: Sensorimotor learning; exploring the world through senses and actions; development of object permanence.
  • Emotional: Expressing basic emotions (joy, anger, fear); formation of initial, deep-rooted attachments.
  • Social: Interactions primarily with parents/caregivers; developing a basic sense of trust vs. mistrust.
Early Childhood

Play & Imagination (2-6 Years)

Expanding language, vivid imagination, and the assertion of independence through structured and unstructured play.

  • Physical: Slower, steadier growth; significant refinement of gross and fine motor skills (running, drawing).
  • Mental: Preoperational thought; language explosion; highly imaginative but egocentric thinking.
  • Emotional: Learning early emotion regulation; beginning to experience complex emotions like guilt and pride.
  • Social: Increasing interest in peer play; asserting autonomy and initiative; learning to share.
Late Childhood

Industry & Mastery (6-12 Years)

Focus shifts dramatically to mastering academic skills, playing structured games, and expanding social circles.

  • Physical: Steady growth; marked improvements in strength, physical coordination, and stamina.
  • Mental: Concrete operational thought; ability to use logic to solve concrete, real-world problems.
  • Emotional: Gaining a sense of competence; self-esteem becomes heavily influenced by peers and academic success.
  • Social: Friendships become increasingly important and complex; peer groups are often same-sex.
Adolescence

Identity & Transition (12-18 Years)

Puberty brings dramatic physical changes accompanied by a deep, challenging search for personal identity.

  • Physical: Onset of puberty leading to sexual maturity; rapid growth spurt; significant prefrontal cortex remodeling.
  • Mental: Emergence of formal operational thought; ability to think abstractly, idealistically, and hypothetically.
  • Emotional: Intense mood shifts influenced by hormones; navigating the complex crisis of establishing a coherent self-identity.
  • Social: Seeking autonomy and psychological distance from parents; extreme reliance on peer groups for validation.
Early Adulthood

Connections & Career (18-40 Years)

Peak physical condition, with a primary focus on establishing intimacy, family structures, and launching careers.

  • Physical: Peak physical performance, strength, and stamina; generally a period of robust health and fertility.
  • Mental: Advanced, pragmatic problem-solving; cognitive focus zeroes in on applying knowledge to career and life goals.
  • Emotional: Navigating the deep desire to form strong, meaningful intimate relationships; learning to balance personal needs with partners.
  • Social: Expanding professional networks; marrying or forming long-term partnerships; often starting families.
Middle Adulthood

Legacy & Generativity (40-65 Years)

A period heavily focused on generativity—raising children, mentoring younger generations, and contributing to society.

  • Physical: Gradual decline in physical capacities (e.g., vision, metabolism); visible signs of aging emerge (wrinkles, graying).
  • Mental: High practical intelligence; crystallized intelligence (accumulated knowledge) peaks; expertise in chosen fields.
  • Emotional: Seeking overarching purpose and meaning in life's work; potentially experiencing a "midlife crisis" or reassessment of goals.
  • Social: Focus shifts to the "sandwich generation" demands: caring for growing children and aging parents simultaneously.
Late Adulthood

Wisdom & Reflection (65+ Years)

Adjusting to the realities of aging and retirement, characterized by reflecting on life overall to attain wisdom.

  • Physical: Noticeable decline in physical strength, sensory acuity, and immune system function; managing chronic conditions.
  • Mental: Potential decline in cognitive processing and reaction speed, but crystallized intelligence (wisdom) remains high.
  • Emotional: Reflecting on life achievements and failures; attaining a sense of integrity and fulfillment over despair.
  • Social: Adjusting to retirement; potential loss of spouses or long-time peers; focusing closely on family legacy and grandchildren.

Key Psychological Theories

Frameworks that explain how we grow cognitively and psychosocially.

Erik Erikson's 8 Stages

Erikson proposed that development consists of an 8-stage lifespan model. At each stage, a person faces a psychosocial crisis. Resolving it leads to a core virtue.

1. Trust vs. Mistrust

Age 0-1 | Virtue: Hope

At this stage babies learn to trust that their parents will meet their basic needs. If a child's basic needs aren't properly met at this age, he or she might grow up with a general mistrust of the world.

Relationship: Maternal Person/Caregiver

Example: An infant crying is fed and comforted, teaching them the world is a safe, dependable place.

2. Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt

Age 2-3 | Virtue: Will

As toddlers, children begin to develop independence and start to learn that they can do some things on their own (such as going to the toilet). If a child is not encouraged properly at this age, he or she might develop shame and doubt about their abilities.

Relationship: Paternal Persons (Parents)

Example: A toddler is allowed to choose their own clothes and dress themselves, developing a sense of independence.

3. Initiative vs. Guilt

Age 4-6 | Virtue: Purpose

As preschoolers, children continue to develop more independence and start to do things of their own initiative. If a child is not able to take initiative and succeed at appropriate tasks, he or she might develop guilt over their needs and desires.

Relationship: Basic Family

Example: A child organizes a game of make-believe with peers, gaining a sense of purpose and leadership.

4. Industry vs. Inferiority

Age 7-12 | Virtue: Competence

Throughout their school years, children continue to develop self-confidence through learning new things. If they are not encouraged and praised properly at this age, they may develop an inferiority complex.

Relationship: Neighborhood, School

Example: A school-age child successfully completes a complex science project, reinforcing feeling of competence.

5. Identity vs. Role Confusion

Age 13-19 | Virtue: Fidelity

When they reach the teenage years, children start to care about how they look to others. They start forming their own identity by experimenting with who they are. If a teenager is unable to properly develop an identity at this age, his or her role confusion will probably continue on into adulthood.

Relationship: Peer Groups, Role Models

Example: A teenager explores different social, political, or career interests before settling on a strong sense of personal identity.

6. Intimacy vs. Isolation

Age 20-34 | Virtue: Love

During early adulthood most people fall in love, get married and start building their own family. If a person is unable to develop intimacy with others at this age (whether through marriage or close friendships), they will probably develop feelings of isolation.

Relationship: Partners, Close Friends

Example: A young adult forms a committed romantic partnership or cultivates a close network of supportive friends.

7. Generativity vs. Stagnation

Age 35-65 | Virtue: Care

This is the longest period of a human's life. It is the stage in which people are usually working and contributing to society in some way and perhaps raising their children. If a person does not find proper ways to be productive during this period, they will probably develop feelings of stagnation.

Relationship: Household, Workmates

Example: A middle-aged adult mentors younger employees at work or raises a child, contributing positively to the next generation.

8. Integrity vs. Despair

Age 65+ | Virtue: Wisdom

As senior citizens, people tend to look back on their lives and think about what they have or have not accomplished. If a person has led a productive life, they will develop a feeling of integrity. If not, they might fall into despair.

Relationship: Mankind, My Kind

Example: An older adult reflects on their life with satisfaction, feeling a deep sense of wisdom and fulfillment despite past struggles.

Abraham Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Maslow proposed that human actions are directed toward goal attainment, organized in a hierarchy from basic physiological needs up to self-actualization.

1. Physiological Needs

Air, water, food, warmth, sleep

Real Life Example: Ensuring you have adequate food and a safe place to sleep before you can focus on making friends or thriving at school.

2. Safety Needs

Job security, financial resources, good health

Real Life Example: Securing a steady job and health insurance to feel financially and physically secure in daily life.

3. Love & Belonging Needs

Friendship, family, intimacy

Real Life Example: Joining a club, sports team, or maintaining close family ties to feel accepted and part of a community.

4. Esteem Needs

Confidence, respect, recognition, fame

Real Life Example: Getting a promotion at work, which provides a profound sense of accomplishment and recognition from peers.

5. Self-Actualization Needs

Achieving one's full potential

Real Life Example: Pursuing a passion project like writing a book or mastering an art form simply for deep inner fulfillment.

Application to Healthcare

Focusing on the unique healthcare needs of Late Adulthood.

Healthcare Considerations for Late Adulthood (65+ Years)

As individuals enter late adulthood, their healthcare needs shift significantly. Medical professionals must adapt their approach to effectively care for this demographic.

  • Chronic Condition Management: High prevalence of chronic diseases (e.g., arthritis, hypertension, diabetes, heart disease). Healthcare shifts to managing these conditions to maintain quality of life.
  • Polypharmacy Risks: Older adults often take multiple medications. Providers must carefully monitor drug interactions and metabolism changes due to aging organs.
  • Fall Prevention & Mobility: Decreased bone density and muscle mass make falls dangerous. Healthcare includes physical therapy, home safety assessments, and mobility aids.
  • Cognitive Monitoring: Routine screening for cognitive decline and Alzheimer's disease becomes critical. Communication must be clear, patient, and often include caregivers or family members.
  • Psychosocial Support: Addressing potential isolation, depression from the loss of peers/spouses, and end-of-life care planning (advance directives).

The Theorist Behind the Hierarchy

A brief biography of Abraham Maslow.

Abraham Harold Maslow

April 1, 1908 – June 8, 1970

Education: Educated at the City College of New York and the University of Wisconsin, where he earned his BA, MA, and PhD in Psychology.

Accomplishments: A pioneer of humanistic psychology. Created Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, profoundly shifting the focus of psychology from abnormal behavior to positive mental health and human potential.

Interesting Fact: Maslow originally studied law at the urging of his parents before switching to psychology to pursue his own interests. He also spent time studying the Blackfoot Indians in Canada, which influenced his ideas regarding self-actualization and social structures.
